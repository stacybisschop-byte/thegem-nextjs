# The Best Sapphire Engagement Rings: 15 Worth Considering

The most famous sapphire engagement ring in the world came from a catalogue. In 1981, Lady Diana Spencer visited Garrard on Regent Street, was shown a selection of pieces, and pointed at an oval Ceylon sapphire surrounded by fourteen round brilliant diamonds in 18-carat white gold. The ring cost £28,000. It is now on Catherine, Princess of Wales's left hand, and it has been on the finger of one royal or another for forty-four years.

The [Garrard story](/stories/garrard-history) is worth knowing. What matters here is the practical consequence: sapphire engagement rings have never been more searched for, more bought, or more varied in what you can get for a given budget. The Diana effect is real and ongoing. What follows is a guide to the best currently available: not the most famous, but the most worth buying across a range of budgets and priorities.

> *In Brief: Sapphire is the second hardest gemstone after diamond, scoring 9 on the Mohs scale, which makes it well suited to daily wear. The stone's colour is the primary value driver: Ceylon (Sri Lanka) sapphires are the cornflower blue of the Wales ring; Burmese sapphires run darker and richer; Kashmir sapphires are the rarest and most expensive of all, with a distinctive velvety quality that no other source replicates. Heat treatment is standard and acceptable; unheated stones of equivalent quality command significant premiums. Budget under £3,000 should look at Ceylon stones from reputable UK retailers. Budget over £5,000 should look at certified natural stones with origin reports.*

## What makes a sapphire worth buying

Before the list, three things that determine whether a sapphire engagement ring is good value or merely adequately priced.

**Colour is everything.** Sapphire value is driven almost entirely by colour quality, not clarity. The ideal is an intense, vivid blue — not so light it washes out, not so dark it appears black in indoor light. The trade term "cornflower blue" describes the mid-range that reads as pure blue in all lighting conditions. Ceylon sapphires from Sri Lanka are the benchmark for this colour. Burmese sapphires tend toward a deeper, more saturated royal blue. Kashmir sapphires have a velvety, slightly milky quality that is entirely distinct and considered the most valuable of all sources.

**Heat treatment is normal.** Almost all commercially available sapphires have been heat-treated to improve colour and clarity. This is industry standard, universally accepted, and does not diminish value in the way that treatment does for other stones. What matters is disclosure. Unheated sapphires of equivalent quality command premiums of 30 to 50 percent over treated equivalents, sometimes more for fine Burmese or Kashmir stones. If a seller cannot tell you whether a stone has been heated, look elsewhere.

**Origin certificates matter at the top end.** For sapphires above approximately £5,000, an origin report from a recognised laboratory (GRS, Gübelin, AGL, or GIA) is worth having. Origin reports verify not just quality but source, which directly affects value. A Burmese sapphire with a GRS certificate commands a different price than an unverified stone of similar appearance.

### For a bespoke ring with full stone specification

**[Vashi](https://www.vashi.com)** is the strongest option for a fully customised sapphire ring at a transparent price. The platform allows you to select stone shape, carat weight, colour intensity, and setting independently, with quality photography and stone specifications clearly published. Their Ceylon sapphire selection runs from approximately £800 for a 1-carat oval in a simple platinum setting to £6,000 and above for a 3-carat stone in a three-stone setting with diamond shoulders. The Diana-influenced oval cluster (sapphire centre, diamond halo) is available across their range and is among the better executions I have seen at the mid-market price point.

**[Brilliant Earth](https://www.brilliantearth.com/engagement-rings/gemstone/sapphire/)** offers ethically sourced natural and lab-created sapphires with full provenance documentation. Their natural sapphire range starts around £1,200 for smaller stones and their three-stone sapphire and diamond settings in platinum are particularly strong in the £3,000 to £5,000 range. Lab-created sapphires are optically identical to natural but cost roughly 40 percent less and are worth serious consideration for buyers prioritising stone size over natural origin.

**[Austen & Blake](https://www.austenblake.com/engagement-rings/sapphire)** handles the mid-market UK bespoke category well, with every ring made to order and strong customisation across setting style and metal. The oval sapphire with diamond cluster is the most searched sapphire engagement ring style by a significant margin, and most versions available are adequate rather than excellent. Two that are better than adequate:

**Pragnell** on Regent Street makes bespoke Diana-influenced cluster rings to a quality level that most online retailers cannot match. They work with Ceylon sapphires exclusively for this style, source stones individually, and their workshop in Stratford-upon-Avon produces rings that hold up over decades rather than years. Expect to pay £4,000 to £12,000 for this level of execution. The in-store appointment process is necessary; this is not an online purchase.

**[Leibish & Co](https://www.leibish.com/collections/sapphire-engagement-rings)** carries certified natural sapphires with GRS and GIA documentation, including cushion and oval cuts with diamond halos that reference the cluster aesthetic without directly copying it. Their pricing is transparent and the stone quality documentation is thorough. Useful for buyers who want a certified natural stone and prefer selecting from an existing inventory rather than commissioning bespoke.

If the Diana ring is one reference point, the other is the Art Deco sapphire — the calibré-cut sapphire borders, the geometric platinum settings, the style that was dominant before the 1920s gave way to the mid-century diamond solitaire.

This is the right source for a buyer who wants an actual Art Deco sapphire ring rather than a contemporary piece designed to evoke the period.

### For a coloured sapphire ring

Blue is the default, but sapphires occur in almost every colour except red (which becomes a ruby). Three alternatives worth knowing:

**Pink sapphire** has become the most popular coloured sapphire alternative to blue, driven partly by its visual proximity to morganite and pink tourmaline and partly by its stronger hardness relative to those alternatives. Vashi and Brilliant Earth both carry pink sapphire options; the colour range runs from pale rose to deep fuchsia, and the strong saturated pinks are the most desirable.

**Padparadscha sapphire** is the rarest variety: an orange-pink sapphire of Sri Lankan origin, named from the Sanskrit for lotus blossom. The colour is distinctive and unlike anything else in fine jewellery. Genuine padparadscha sapphires with origin certification are expensive and rare; pieces described as padparadscha without certification are frequently pink sapphires or pink-orange spinels. Leibish carries certified padparadscha stones occasionally; alert sellers to search when available rather than browsing standing inventory.

**Yellow sapphire** has been growing steadily in popularity as an alternative to yellow diamond at a fraction of the price. The strong canary yellows are the most desirable. Vashi and Brilliant Earth both carry yellow sapphire options; the price differential versus a yellow diamond of comparable appearance is significant.

## What to check before buying

**Ask for the heat treatment status.** Every reputable seller will know. "We don't have that information" is not an acceptable answer for a sapphire purchase above approximately £1,000.

**Check the colour in multiple light conditions.** A sapphire that looks ideal under a retailer's display lighting may read too dark or too purple in natural daylight. If buying online, request daylight photographs or video. If buying in person, take the stone to the door.

**Understand the setting's effect on colour.** White gold and platinum settings make blue sapphires appear brighter and more saturated. Yellow gold settings create a warmer, more vintage quality and can make the blue appear slightly greenish in some stones. Neither is wrong, but the effect is significant and worth knowing before choosing your metal.

**For stones above £3,000, ask for a laboratory report.** GIA, GRS, or AGL are the recognised authorities. The report gives you an independent colour grade, clarity assessment, and (for stones above a certain value) an origin opinion. It also substantially aids resale if you ever sell.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why are sapphire engagement rings so popular?

Sapphire engagement rings have experienced sustained popularity since Princess Diana's 12-carat Ceylon sapphire cluster became the most recognised engagement ring in the world in 1981. The ring passed to Catherine, Princess of Wales in 2010 and remains consistently one of the most searched jewellery pieces globally. Beyond the royal association, sapphires offer a compelling practical case: they score 9 on the Mohs hardness scale, making them the most durable coloured gemstone for daily wear, and their colour palette extends well beyond blue into pink, yellow, orange, and padparadscha.

### How much does a sapphire engagement ring cost?

A Ceylon sapphire engagement ring in a simple setting starts around £800 to £1,500 from reputable UK online retailers. A well-specified 1 to 2-carat oval Ceylon sapphire in a platinum cluster setting with diamond surround runs £2,500 to £6,000. Fine natural Burmese sapphires with GRS certification begin around £8,000 for smaller stones and increase significantly with size and quality. Kashmir sapphires are among the most expensive gemstones in the world; fine examples sell at Christie's and Sotheby's for £30,000 to £200,000 and above.

### What is a Ceylon sapphire?

Ceylon sapphire is the trade name for sapphires from Sri Lanka, the source of the Diana/Wales ring and the benchmark for the cornflower blue colour most associated with the best quality. Ceylon sapphires are typically a vivid, medium-toned blue that reads as pure blue in all light conditions. They are the most widely available quality sapphire and offer the best balance of quality and accessibility in the engagement ring market.

### Is Kashmir sapphire worth the premium?

Kashmir sapphires are produced by a deposit in the Zanskar range of the Himalayas that was largely exhausted by the 1920s. The characteristic velvety quality of Kashmir sapphires — a slight milkiness in the blue that gives the stone a soft, three-dimensional appearance — is not replicated by any other source. For buyers who can verify origin through a GRS or Gübelin report, genuine Kashmir sapphires have shown consistent appreciation in value and are among the most defensible jewellery investments available. The premium over comparable Ceylon stones is typically three to five times.

### What is the difference between heated and unheated sapphires?

Almost all commercially available sapphires have been heat-treated at high temperatures to improve colour and remove inclusions. This process is industry standard, permanent, stable, and fully accepted in the trade. Unheated sapphires of equivalent quality command premiums because natural untreated colour is rarer than treated colour. For most buyers, a heat-treated sapphire of excellent colour is a better purchase than an unheated stone of mediocre colour. For investment purposes or for buyers interested in the finest stones, unheated status with laboratory certification is significant.
