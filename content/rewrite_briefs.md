# Rewrite Briefs — Migration Content

These 15 briefs cover the pages migrating from gemstonesinsider.com to the new publication. Each existing article has already earned some SEO equity; the rewrite job is to keep what works (target keywords, factual depth, ranking strength) while replacing what doesn't (encyclopedia voice, dated framing, AI-flavoured prose, weak commercial integration).

All rewrites should land in the editorial voice defined in the masthead style guide: Vogue/GQ meets Atlas Obscura, written as if by a 38-year-old features editor living near the King's Road — curious, dry, authoritative without being pompous, no AI tells (no "delve," no "tapestry," no tidy reflective endings, no "it's not just X, it's Y").

For shared reference: the launch dozen briefs (Princess Diana, Elizabeth Taylor, Hope Diamond, Lab vs Natural Diamonds, etc.) are separate and cover **new** content being written for launch. These 15 briefs cover **existing** content being migrated with rewrites.

**New angle:** Frame around the auction. That tension (what she owned vs what we remember her wearing) is the editorial spine.

**Structure:** Open at the auction. ### Brief M3 — How Cartier Became Cartier

**Target keywords:** *cartier history, cartier family, cartier brand history, louis cartier, jacques cartier, pierre cartier*.

**New angle:** Three brothers, three continents. Louis took Paris, Jacques took London, Pierre took New York. They built one of the only luxury houses where the founders' decisions still shape what the brand makes today. The Tutti Frutti collection came from Jacques's trips to India in 1911. Close on the brand today — sold to Richemont in 1972, but the family-era designs are still 90% of what people buy. The story has a moral about why founder-led brands keep their soul longer.

**Word count:** 2,200–2,800.

**Affiliates:** None — this is editorial credibility, not commerce. Internal link to the Tiffany piece (parallel brand stories) and Marilyn (Cartier appears in her jewellery story).

### Brief M11 — What Counts as Precious? (Precious vs Semi-Precious)

**Old URL:** `/semi-precious-gemstones/` → **New URL:** `/guides/precious-vs-semi-precious/`

**Current performance:** **132 clicks, 5,482 impressions, position 5.39**. The biggest non-brand click driver on the entire site. Don't break the keyword positioning.

**Diagnosis:** The existing piece is presumably encyclopedic — list of semi-precious stones with descriptions. Reframe as an editorial guide that takes a position: the precious/semi-precious distinction is historically arbitrary and increasingly meaningless, and here's why that matters when you're buying. Keep the keyword density on "semi-precious gemstones" — that's the load-bearing query.

**Target keywords:** *semi-precious gemstones, semi-precious stones, precious vs semi-precious, what are semi-precious gemstones, list of semi-precious stones*.

**New angle:** The distinction was invented in the mid-nineteenth century by French gemologist Léonce Mascheleyn and was always more about marketing than mineralogy. Only four stones get to be "precious" — diamond, ruby, sapphire, emerald — and the cut-off is essentially Victorian convention. Today, a fine paraiba tourmaline sells for more per carat than most rubies, a top-quality red spinel beats lower-grade rubies, and the distinction tells you almost nothing about value or beauty. The piece both explains the history and gives the practical guidance: when does the "semi-precious" label actually matter (mostly: insurance, customs, and certain auction categories) and when can the buyer ignore it.

**Structure:** Open with a counterfactual ("Tanzanite was discovered in 1967. It's officially 'semi-precious.' A top tanzanite outsells most sapphires per carat. Discuss."). Then the history of the distinction; the four "precious" stones and why; the major "semi-precious" stones that now command higher prices than their precious counterparts (tanzanite, paraiba tourmaline, fine spinel, demantoid garnet, some sapphires-that-aren't-sapphire); the practical implications for buyers (insurance valuations, certification, resale); a closing rule of thumb.

**Word count:** 2,200–2,800.

**Affiliates:** Heavy. Brilliant Earth, James Allen, Etsy curated sellers. Inline links per stone discussed.

**Internal links:** Engagement Ring Guide (alternative stones), Lab vs Natural Diamonds.

**Pinterest:** 4 pins. The comparison-chart pin will travel.

## Process notes

**For all migrations:**

1. **Read the existing piece carefully** before writing the rewrite. Identify the keywords that earned position (use Search Console data + the keyword report) and the factual specifics worth preserving. These are SEO load-bearing.

2. **Match or exceed existing word count.** Google has indexed and ranked the existing piece partly on its length. Shorter rewrites can lose position even when the writing is better.

3. **Preserve the H1 and meta title's core keywords.** You can change the wording, but the principal noun and the search intent must remain. "Marilyn Monroe Jewellery" must still appear in the H1 and meta title even if the title is rephrased.

4. **Set up the redirect before publishing.** The 301 from old URL to new URL needs to be live the day the new piece publishes — and the old piece must be removed from the index (a noindex tag plus the redirect, ideally).

5. **Mark `lastReviewedAt`** in Sanity for any piece that should be updated annually (Lab vs Natural Diamonds, Best Jewellery Books, Jewellery Trends).

6. **Voice check before publish:** read aloud. If anywhere it sounds like a press release, an Instagram caption, or a university essay, it's failed. Particular failure modes for AI-assisted drafts: stacked tricolons, em-dash overuse, "It's not just X, it's Y" constructions, tidy reflective summary endings, the words *delve, tapestry, realm, intersection, journey, vibrant, iconic, robust*.

7. **Photography is the differentiator.** A Stories piece without proper imagery isn't a Stories piece. Budget appropriately or hold the article until imagery is sorted.
