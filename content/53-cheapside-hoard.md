# The Cheapside Hoard: London's Greatest Lost Treasure

On 18 June 1912, a workman swinging a pickaxe through the floor of a cellar at 30–32 Cheapside struck something that wasn't rubble. The building above was being demolished; it was a timber-framed structure at the corner of Cheapside and Friday Street, scheduled for redevelopment. The cellar beneath was brick-lined, floored in chalk, and hadn't been opened in approximately two hundred and seventy years. What the pickaxe found was a decayed wooden box. What the box contained was nearly five hundred pieces of Elizabethan and Jacobean jewellery.

The gems spilled onto the cellar floor. The workman, unacquainted with what he was looking at, gathered what he could into his pockets and his handkerchief. He and his colleagues sold the contents over the following days and weeks to a man they knew as "Stony Jack" — George Fabian Lawrence, an antiques dealer and pawnbroker who had established a standing arrangement with demolition crews across the city. He paid them cash or pints of beer for interesting finds. Lawrence had become Inspector of Excavations at the newly formed London Museum the previous year. His appointment proved well-timed.

Over the weeks that followed, parcels of jewellery arrived at Lawrence's office. The collection he assembled became the Cheapside Hoard: the greatest single collection of Elizabethan and Jacobean jewellery ever found, and one of the most significant archaeological discoveries in London's history.

> *In Brief: The Cheapside Hoard is a collection of nearly five hundred pieces of late sixteenth and early seventeenth century jewellery, discovered beneath a cellar at 30–32 Cheapside in London on 18 June 1912. The hoard was probably the stock of a goldsmith-jeweller buried around 1640, shortly before the Civil War began in 1642. The owner never returned. The collection is now held mostly at the London Museum, with pieces at the British Museum and the V&A. Its gemstones were sourced from every corner of the known world, making the hoard the most complete record of the Elizabethan luxury trade that exists.*

## Where it came from

Cheapside in the early seventeenth century was the City of London's main commercial artery and its ceremonial processional route simultaneously. It was where Elizabeth I rode in triumph. It was also where goldsmiths and jewellers clustered so densely that the street had its own distinct identity as the hub of the luxury trade. Goldsmith's Row occupied much of the southern side of the street, a stretch of buildings famed across Europe for the quality and variety of their wares.

The hoard's location tells you what it was. This was not a private collection hidden by a wealthy owner. The variety and quantity (nearly five hundred pieces across rings, brooches, chains, pendants, loose gemstones, scent bottles, fan holders, crystal tankards, cameos, and a salt cellar) is the inventory of a dealer's stock. Someone who ran a business on Cheapside buried their working stock in the cellar and did not come back for it.

The most likely explanation is the Civil War. The hoard was buried around 1640, two years before hostilities began. London's goldsmiths were affected immediately: the King's supporters and Parliament's forces both had claims on the city's wealth, and a jeweller on Cheapside in 1640 had every reason to secure their stock against confiscation or theft. Whoever buried the hoard left it well hidden. They either died in the conflict, fled the city permanently, or found themselves unable to return for reasons history has not recorded. The cellar remained sealed until 1912.

## The objects

The range of what the hoard contained is staggering in its variety, and the variety is itself the historical evidence. This was a stock assembled to serve every level of the luxury market: from relatively modest rings set with semi-precious stones to objects of extraordinary technical ambition that only the wealthiest clients could have considered.

The most extraordinary single object in the hoard is an emerald watch. The case is made from a Colombian emerald from the Muzo mines, large enough to be hollowed out to house a watch mechanism. The emerald has been cut with the kind of mastery that suggests its lapidary worked in Seville, Lisbon, Geneva, or London: the four cities where that combination of technical skill and access to Colombian rough existed in the early seventeenth century. It is not a large object. It is approximately the size of a fig. It is one of the most technically audacious objects in the history of jewellery.

The rings run to over a hundred pieces, almost half of them taking the form of a rose: a central emerald or garnet surrounded by smaller stones in an enamelled gold setting. The chains include one over a metre long, made from alternating roses, leaves, and floral bows. In the Elizabethan period, such chains were worn in multiple loops reaching to the waist; the fashion required length. There are scent bottles with handles suggesting they were worn from chains. There are cameos, including one carved in agate showing Queen Elizabeth I, of the kind she distributed to courtiers and supporters. There are amethyst grape clusters that match designs in the pattern books of the Dutch jeweller Arnold Lulls, who was working in London between 1584 and 1642. And there are three pieces that appear to be jewellery but are not: glass pastes, rock crystal pieces dressed to resemble balas rubies, the counterfeits of the trade.

## The gemstones as a map

If the objects tell you about Elizabethan taste and technical capacity, the gemstones tell you about the world.

The hoard demonstrates the international trade in luxury goods in the period, including gemstones from sources across South America, Asia, and Europe: emerald from Colombia, topaz and amazonite from Brazil; spinel, iolite, and chrysoberyl from Sri Lanka; Indian diamond; Burmese ruby; Afghan lapis lazuli; Persian turquoise; pearls from Bahrain; peridot from the Red Sea; Bohemian and Hungarian opal, garnet, and amethyst.

Read that list slowly. It is a complete map of the known world's gem-producing regions, assembled in a single dealer's stock on a single London street in the early seventeenth century. The Colombian emeralds came via Spain, which controlled the Muzo mines. The rubies came through the Indian Ocean trade routes. The lapis lazuli from Afghanistan would have crossed Persia and the Ottoman Empire before reaching a London merchant. The pearls from Bahrain — the Persian Gulf's great pearl fisheries — would have taken a similar route. The diamonds are Indian, from the Golconda mines that produced every significant diamond in Europe before the Brazilian fields were discovered in the 1720s.

What the hoard reveals, in other words, is not just what one London jeweller had in stock. It reveals the extraordinary reach of early seventeenth century trade; the degree to which a city of perhaps two hundred thousand people was already at the centre of a truly global luxury market.

## Stony Jack and the acquisition

The story of how the hoard entered public ownership is only marginally less remarkable than the story of the hoard itself.

George Fabian Lawrence was fifty-two years old in 1912, a dealer who had spent decades cultivating relationships with demolition and construction workers across London. His method was simple and effective: he made himself known to the labourers, paid reliably, and made clear that nothing was too small or too damaged to be worth bringing to him. "I taught them that every scrap of metal, pottery, glass, or leather that has been lying under London may have a story to tell the archaeologist, and is worth saving," he told a newspaper in 1937, near the end of his life.

His appointment as Inspector of Excavations at the London Museum in 1911 gave him an institutional purpose for what had previously been a personal enterprise. The Cheapside discovery, in June of his first year in the role, was the culmination of decades of groundwork. The jewels came to him in pockets and handkerchiefs, over days and weeks, as the workmen cleared the site.

No treasure trove inquest was held. The Goldsmiths' Company, whose historical jurisdiction over Cheapside was long established, did not assert ownership. Lewis Vernon Harcourt, 1st Viscount Harcourt, provided the funds for the London Museum to purchase the bulk of the collection. Twenty-five pieces went to the British Museum, five to the V&A. One gold and enamel chain was purchased separately by the V&A. The rest, the great majority, went to the London Museum, where it remains.

## What the hoard tells us

The Cheapside Hoard is a historical document as much as a jewellery collection. It tells us what the Elizabethan luxury trade actually looked like from the inside: not the crown jewels, not the portraiture diamonds worn by queens and kept in royal collections, but the working stock of a commercial jeweller serving a broad clientele.

It tells us that the market included counterfeits alongside fine stones, which means there was a market for stones that looked expensive without being so. It tells us that the same dealer stocked Colombian emeralds and Bohemian garnets, Indian diamonds and Persian turquoise, at multiple price points for multiple clients. It tells us that English lapidary craft in the period was capable of work (the emerald watch, the carved amethyst grapes, the rock crystal tankards) that stands comparison with anything produced anywhere in the world at that time.

And it tells us, through the simple fact of its concealment, that whoever buried it expected to return and did not. The hoard was not a deliberate bequest. It was a practical decision by someone who believed the danger would pass. The Civil War lasted from 1642 to 1651. Whatever happened to the jeweller of Cheapside in those years, they did not come back to Friday Street.

The collection is now in the London Museum, which moved in 2025 to the former Smithfield General Market building and is in the process of reinstallation as of 2026. The emerald watch, the rose rings, the amethyst grapes, and the agate cameo of Queen Elizabeth I will be on display when the new galleries open. They are worth going to see. Nothing else like them exists.

## Frequently asked questions

### Where is the Cheapside Hoard now?

The majority of the Cheapside Hoard is held by the London Museum (formerly the Museum of London), which moved in 2025 to the former Smithfield General Market building in the City of London. Twenty-five pieces are at the British Museum and five are at the Victoria and Albert Museum, which also holds one gold and enamel chain purchased separately at the time of acquisition. The London Museum's new galleries, currently being installed as of 2026, will include significant pieces from the hoard.

### Who found the Cheapside Hoard?

The hoard was discovered on 18 June 1912 by workmen demolishing a timber-framed building at 30–32 Cheapside. The workmen sold the pieces to George Fabian Lawrence, an antiques dealer and pawnbroker known as "Stony Jack" who paid demolition workers across London for archaeological finds. Lawrence had been appointed Inspector of Excavations at the London Museum in 1911; the Cheapside discovery was the most significant of his career. No treasure trove inquest was held, and the Goldsmiths' Company did not assert ownership.

### Why was the Cheapside Hoard buried?

The hoard is believed to have been buried around 1640, two years before the English Civil War began in 1642. The location, on Cheapside in the heart of London's goldsmith trade, suggests it was the working stock of a goldsmith-jeweller who buried it for safekeeping — most likely in anticipation of the violence and confiscation that accompanied the Civil War. The owner never returned to retrieve it. The building above the cellar was destroyed in the Great Fire of London in 1666 and rebuilt by the Goldsmiths' Company the following year; the cellar below, with its wooden box, remained sealed until 1912.

### What is the most valuable piece in the Cheapside Hoard?

The most technically extraordinary piece is the emerald watch: a timepiece whose case is made from a single Colombian emerald from the Muzo mines, hollowed out to house a watch mechanism. It is considered one of the most technically ambitious objects in the history of jewellery. The hoard also includes a Colombian emerald scent bottle, numerous rings with significant stones, and over a hundred chains. Financial valuation of individual pieces is complicated by the collection's institutional ownership and historical significance.

### What can you learn from the Cheapside Hoard?

The hoard provides the most complete surviving record of the Elizabethan and Jacobean luxury trade. Its gemstones were sourced from Colombia, Brazil, Sri Lanka, India, Burma, Afghanistan, Persia, Bahrain, the Red Sea, and Bohemia, demonstrating the global reach of London's early seventeenth century commercial networks. The range of objects, from modest garnet rings to the emerald watch, maps the full commercial spectrum of the period's jewellery market. The presence of glass pastes and rock crystal pieces designed to imitate precious stones shows that the market included affordable imitations alongside genuine gems.
